About Ghanaian Pastries

Ghanaian pastries reflect the country's blend of culture and tradition. From sweet fried dough to spicy meat-filled pies, each delicacy tells a story of heritage passed through generations. Passed down through oral tradition, most of these snacks were created as part of celebrations and communal gatherings, made with locally available ingredients.

Featured Local Pastries

Bofrot (Puff-Puff)

Origin: A festive fried dough snack common in West Africa.

Ingredients: Flour, yeast, sugar, water, salt, oil.

Steps:

  1. Mix dry ingredients and water.
  2. Let dough rise for 1-2 hours.
  3. Deep-fry in hot oil until golden brown.

Koose

Origin: Savory black-eyed pea fritters eaten across the North and Zongo communities.

Ingredients: Black-eyed peas, onions, peppers, salt, oil.

Steps:

  1. Blend soaked beans with spices.
  2. Whisk to incorporate air.
  3. Fry until crispy brown.

Ghanaian Meat Pie

Origin: Colonial-era pastry with British influence, adapted with Ghanaian spices.

Ingredients: Flour, margarine, salt, water, minced meat, onions.

Steps:

  1. Prepare dough and filling separately.
  2. Stuff pastry with meat filling.
  3. Bake until golden brown.

Poloo (Fried Coconut Dough)

Origin: A crunchy coconut-infused snack served during holidays and festive seasons.

Ingredients: Flour, sugar, salt, nutmeg, grated coconut, water.

Steps:

  1. Mix all ingredients into a soft dough.
  2. Shape into strips or rings.
  3. Fry until golden and crispy.

Atwemo (Twisted Fried Dough)

Origin: Traditional festive snack made by the Akan people for special occasions.

Ingredients: Flour, nutmeg, eggs, sugar, salt, margarine.

Steps:

  1. Combine dry and wet ingredients.
  2. Twist dough into shapes.
  3. Fry until crisp and light brown.

Nkate Cake (Peanut Brittle)

Origin:Nkate Cake is a crunchy Ghanaian snack made by street vendors, especially near schools and markets. It reflects a fusion of indigenous peanut use with sugar introduced during trade eras .

Ingredients: 2 cups roasted peanuts ,1 cup granulated sugar ,1 tsp water ,Pinch of salt

Steps:

  1. Roast peanuts if not already roasted, and remove skins.
  2. In a saucepan, melt sugar with a tiny bit of water over low heat.
  3. Stir continuously until sugar melts and turns golden brown.
  4. Quickly add peanuts and mix until coated.
  5. Pour onto a greased surface or tray and press flat with a rolling pin.
  6. Cut into squares before it hardens.

Kube Cake (Coconut Candy)

Origin: Kube Cake is a chewy, caramel-like candy made from coconut, commonly sold in school canteens and by local vendors. It's popular in coastal communities where coconuts are abundant.

Ingredients: 1 large coconut (grated) , 1 cup of sugar, 1/4 cup water ,Optional: condensed milk or ginger.

Steps:

  1. Grate the coconut flesh.
  2. In a pan, melt sugar and water over medium heat.
  3. When a syrup forms, add coconut and stir continuously.
  4. Cook until the mixture thickens and browns slightly.
  5. Spoon onto greased tray or parchment to cool.
  6. Let it harden and serve.

Ayigbe Biscuit

Origin: From the Volta Region, this biscuit is crunchy and gluten-free, made with cassava dough — a staple in Ewe communities.

Ingredients: 2 cups of cassava dough, sugar,Grated coconut (optional)

Steps:

  1. Mix cassava dough and sugar into a stiff paste.
  2. Add grated coconut (optional).
  3. Shape into small biscuits.
  4. Bake in a hot oven or traditional clay oven until cripsy.

Tatale (Plantain Pancakes)

Origin:Tatale originates from the Akan people. It’s a sweet-savoury plantain dish commonly paired with beans (called “Red Red”).

Ingredients:2 overripe plantains, 1/4 cup of cornmeal or flour, onion, pepper, salt,Spices (ginger).

Steps:

  1. Mash plaintains in a bowl.
  2. Add chopped onion, pepper, cornmeal, and spices.
  3. Mix into a batter-like consistency.
  4. Heat oil and fry in scoops like pancakes.
  5. Cook both sides until brown.
