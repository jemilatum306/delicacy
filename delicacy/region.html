A Taste of Ghana's Diversity

Ghana's culinary landscape is as diverse as its people. Each region boasts unique dishes shaped by local ingredients, cultural influences, and traditional cooking methods. This interactive guide showcases the most representative dishes from each of Ghana's regions.

Explore Ghana's Regional Delicacies

Each region in Ghana offers unique flavors and cooking styles. Try these dishes to taste the diversity of Ghanaian cuisine!

Northern Region Delicacies

Discover some popular dishes amomg the Dagomba's and other ethnic groups in the north.

Tuo Zaafi with Ayoyo stew

Ingredients

  • Corn flour or millet flour
  • Jute leaves (Ayoyo)
  • Dawadawa (local seasoning)
  • Peppers
  • Onions
  • Tomatoes
  • Meat or fish
  • Salt
  • Mix corn flour with water to form a smooth paste
  • Cook while stirring continuously until thickened (Tuo zaafi)
  • Wash and chop jute leaves
  • Boil meat or fish with dawadawa seasioning
  • Blend onions , tomatoes and pepper
  • Combine blended ingredients, mix with cooked meat and jute leaves
  • Simmer until soup thickens

Origin Story:

Tuo Zaafi, also known as T.Z., originates from the Hausa language. The name itself combines "Tuo," meaning stirring or paddling, and "Zaafi," meaning hot. It is a popular dish in Northern Ghana, and also widely enjoyed in other West African countries like Nigeria, Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger. Tuo Zaafi is considered a nutritious and easily digestible meal.

Wasawasa

Ingredients

  • Yam
  • Pepper
  • Onions
  • Salt
  • Tomatoes
  • Eggs (optional)
  • Peel and grate raw yam
  • Allow grated yam to ferment for 1-2 days
  • Mix fermented yam with salt
  • Scoop into portions and steam until set
  • Prepare sauce with blended peppers , onions and tomatoes
  • Serve wasawasa with sauce

Origin Story:

Wasawasa, a traditional dish from northern Ghana, particularly among the Dagbon people, is made from dried yam peels ground into flour and steamed. Its origin story is rooted in resourcefulness and a desire to minimize food waste. A legend says that a woman, initially throwing away yam peels, realized they could be used to make a nutritious meal, thus creating wasawasa.

Ashanti Delicacies

Home to legendary dishes like fufu and light soup with rich, comforting flavors.

Fufu and light soup

What You'll Need:

  • Plantains and cassava (for fufu)
  • Tomatoes
  • Onions
  • Garlic
  • Ginger
  • Garden eggs
  • Meat (goat,beef,or fish)
  • Salt and spices
  • Peel and boil plantains and cassava until soft
  • Pound in a mortar to make smooth fufu dough
  • Blend tomatoes, onions, garlic, and ginger for the soup base
  • Cook meat with spices until tender
  • Combine blended ingredients with meat and simmer
  • Serve fufu with soup in a bowl

The Story Behind This Dish:

The word "Fufu" is believed to be derived from the Twi language, meaning "white," likely referring to the whitish color of the cassava component. Fufu, a starchy dough, is traditionally made by pounding boiled cassava and plantains together. Light Soup, a flavorful broth, is known for its thin consistency and is traditionally tomato-based, often featuring seafood. The dish is a staple, especially for Sunday meals, and is enjoyed by many across Ghana and beyond.

Nkontomire (Palava Sauce)

What You'll Need:

  • Cocoyam leaves (kontomire)
  • Palm oil
  • Onions
  • Tomatoes
  • Eggs
  • Fish (smoked or salted)
  • Peppers
  • Salt and spices
  • Wash and chop cocoyam leaves finely
  • Boil eggs and set aside
  • Fry onions, tomatoes, and peppers in palm oil
  • Add chopped leaves and cook until tender
  • Add flaked fish and boiled eggs
  • Season to taste and simmer for flavors to blend

The Story Behind This Dish:

Kontomire stew, also known as Palava Sauce, is a popular Ghanaian dish made with cocoyam leaves (kontomire), often featuring a mix of meats, smoked fish, and other ingredients. The name "Palava Sauce" is said to have originated in Elmina, Ghana, and is thought to be linked to the English word "palaver," referring to a lively or even argumentative discussion. The dish is known for its rich, flavorful taste and is commonly enjoyed with various sides like boiled yam, plantain, and rice. Kontomire stew can be made with different variations, such as adding agushi seeds, or with different types of fish. It's a versatile dish that can be adapted to individual preferences and regional variations.

Mpoto Mpoto (Yam Pottage)

What You'll Need:

  • Yam
  • Palm oil
  • Onions
  • Tomatoes
  • Peppers
  • Fish or meat
  • Salt and spices
  • Peel and cut yam into chunks
  • Boil yam until soft but not mushy
  • Prepare sauce separately with palm oil, onions, tomatoes, and peppers
  • Add fish or meat to sauce and simmer
  • Add boiled yam chunks to sauce and mash slightly
  • Cook together briefly until flavors meld

The Story Behind This Dish:

Mpoto Mpoto, also known as Yam Pottage or Asaro, is a popular Ghanaian dish, particularly among the Akan people. It's a simple yet flavorful one-pot meal made with yam or cocoyam, often cooked with palm oil, onions, peppers, and various spices, sometimes including smoked fish. The dish is known for its creamy texture and is enjoyed hot or warm.

Central Region Delicacies

Specializes in fresh seafood dishes and coastal flavors.

Kenkey and Fried Fish

Ingredients:

  • Corn dough
  • Fish (typically tilapia)
  • Peppers
  • Onions
  • Tomatoes
  • Salt
  • Oil for frying
  • Dry banana leaves for wrapping
  • Ferment corn dough partially
  • Mix portion of dough with boiling water to form 'kata'
  • Combine with remaining dough and wrap in banana leaves
  • Steam wrapped dough for several hours
  • Clean and season fish
  • Prepare hot pepper sauce with onions and tomatoes
  • Fry fish until crispy

Origin Story:

Kenkey and fried fish is a classic Ghanaian dish, with kenkey (also known as kɔmi or dokono) originating from the Ga and Fante communities of Ghana. Kenkey is a type of fermented corn dough dumpling, and fried fish is a common accompaniment, often paired with a spicy pepper sauce (shito).

Ampesi with Kontomire

Ingredients:

  • Cocoyam leaves
  • Yam
  • Plantains
  • Palm oil
  • Onions
  • Peppers
  • Smoked fish
  • Salt
  • Peel and cut yam and plantains into chunks
  • Boil in salted water until tender
  • Wash and chop cocoyam leaves
  • Sauté onions and peppers in palm oil
  • Add chopped leaves and cook until tender
  • Flake smoked fish into sauce
  • Serve boiled yam and plantains with kontomire sauce

Origin Story:

Ampesi with Kontomire is a traditional Ghanaian dish with roots in the Akan culture. Ampesi refers to the boiled starchy foods like plantain, yam, or cocoyam, while Kontomire is a stew made from cocoyam leaves (taro leaves). The dish is a staple in many Ghanaian homes and is known for its nutritional value and simple preparation.

Volta Region Delicacies

Discover features staples like Akple and traditional Ewe dishes.

Akple with Okro Soup

What You'll Need:

  • Corn dough
  • Cassava dough
  • Okro (okra)
  • Fish or Crab(optional)
  • Onions and pepper
  • Palm oil
  • Salt
  • Mix corn and cassava dough with water
  • Cook mixture into stiff paste (Akple)
  • Slice okro into small pieces
  • Prepare fish and crab
  • Sauté onions and peppers in palm oil
  • Add okro, fish and crab with some water
  • Cook until slimy consistency is achieved

The Story Behind This Dish:

Akple with okra soup is a traditional Ghanaian dish originating from the Ewe people of the Volta Region. The Ewe are known for this dish, which is also enjoyed by other ethnic groups in Ghana and parts of Togo. Akple is made from a dough of fermented corn and cassava flour, while okra soup is a flavorful stew made with okra pods, vegetables, and often meat or fish. The combination of Akple and okra soup is a staple at Ewe cultural events and family gatherings.

Dzenkple

What You'll Need:

  • Corn flour
  • Smoked fish
  • Shrimp and crap
  • Tomatoes and onions
  • Ginger and garlic
  • Salt and seasoning
  • Coconut milk
  • The stew is made by sautéing onions and other aromatics, then adding blended tomatoes, tomato paste, and the spice blend.
  • Shrimp and crabs are often seasoned and cooked separately or added directly to the stew.
  • The corn flour is added to the stew, often after it has been cooked down and thickened, and stirred until it forms a porridge-like consistency.
  • Coconut milk, water, and additional seasonings are added to the dzenkple before serving.

The Story Behind This Dish:

Dzenkple is a traditional Ghanaian dish, particularly popular among the Ewe people of the Volta Region and neighboring areas in Togo, Benin, and Nigeria. It's a festive meal featuring roasted corn flour cooked in a rich tomato stew, often mistaken for Aprapransa, which uses palmnut soup. Dzenkple is known for its distinctive tomato-based stew.

Yakeyake

What You'll Need

  • Cassava
  • Salt
  • Corn flour (optional)
  • Pepper/stew or soup
  • Makaya leaves(optional)
  • Peel and grate the cassava. Some recipes suggest soaking the grated cassava to remove toxins.
  • The grated cassava is then squeezed or pressed to remove excess water.
  • Salt is added to the grated cassava, and the mixture is steamed, traditionally using a muslin cloth or a special earthenware bowl with holes.
  • Once cooked, Yakeyake is often served with a stew, pepper sauce, or a fish broth

The Story Behind The Dish

Yakeyake, a steamed cassava dish, is a culinary staple primarily associated with the Ewe people of the Volta Region in Ghana. It's a testament to the region's rich culinary heritage and is known for its unique preparation method and cultural significance.

Eastern Region Delicacies

Home to the Akyem and Kwahu people, the Eastern Region offers earthy flavors with staples like cassava, palm nuts, and fresh river fish.

Fufu with Abenkwan

Ingredients:

  • For Fufu: Cassava & plantains (2:1 ratio)
  • For Abenkwan: Fresh palm nuts (or canned cream), smoked fish (e.g., salmon or mackerel), garden eggs, onions, garlic, ginger, habanero peppers, salt
  • Fufu: Boil cassava/plantains until soft, pound in a mortar until smooth.
  • Abenkwan: Boil palm nuts for 30 mins, extract thick cream by pressing through a sieve.
  • Sauté blended onions, garlic, and ginger in palm oil. Add palm cream, smoked fish, and garden eggs.
  • Simmer for 20 mins until oily sheen forms. Serve with fufu.

Cultural Significance:

Abenkwan is a ceremonial dish among the Akyem people, often served at weddings and funerals. The soup’s richness symbolizes prosperity. Historically, palm nuts were traded as currency in pre-colonial Ghana.

Akyeke

Ingredients:

  • Fermented cassava (grated, soaked for 3 days)
  • Salt
  • For Sauce: Palm oil, smoked fish, tomatoes, onions, shrimp powder
  • Rinse fermented cassava to reduce sourness, mix with salt.
  • Steam in banana leaves for 45 mins until firm.
  • Sauce: Fry onions in palm oil, add tomatoes, smoked fish, and shrimp powder. Simmer for 10 mins.
  • Serve akyeke warm with sauce.

Historical Context:

Akyeke originated with the Akyem farmers as a way to preserve cassava during harvest seasons. The fermentation process breaks down cyanide compounds in raw cassava, making it safe to eat.

Nkatenkwan

Ingredients:

  • Peanut paste (unsweetened)
  • Chicken or goat meat
  • Tomatoes, onions, garlic, ginger
  • Garden eggs (optional)
  • Dawadawa (fermented locust beans)
  • Boil meat with onions, garlic, and ginger until tender.
  • Blend tomatoes and peppers, sauté in palm oil. Add peanut paste and 2 cups of water.
  • Stir continuously to prevent lumps. Add meat, garden eggs, and dawadawa.
  • Simmer for 15 mins. Serve with rice balls (omo tuo).

Origin Story:

Nkatenkwan (from "nkate" = peanut, "nkwan" = soup) was introduced by the Bono people but adopted by the Akyem. It’s a protein-rich dish traditionally fed to nursing mothers.

Greater Accra Region Delicacies

The Greater Accra Region, home to the Ga-Dangme people, is famous for its rich coastal cuisine. Fresh seafood, fermented corn, and peppery sauces are central to their food culture.

Kenkey with Fried Fish and Shito

Ingredients:

  • Fermented corn dough
  • Salt and water
  • Fresh tilapia or mackerel
  • Shito (black pepper sauce)
  • Sliced onions and tomatoes (optional)
  • Vegetable oil, pepper, and seasonings
  • Mix fermented corn dough with water and partially cook it (aflata)
  • Wrap in dried corn husks and boil for 2–3 hours
  • Clean and season fish, then fry until crispy
  • Prepare shito: blend onions, pepper, tomato, fry in oil with dried fish powder and spices
  • Serve kenkey hot with fried fish, shito, and optional fresh veggies

Origin Story:

Ga Kenkey (also called "Komé") is a staple among the Ga people in Accra. It's made from fermented corn dough, a reflection of the Ga-Dangme community’s knowledge of fermentation and preservation. Shito and fried fish complement the dish, making it a popular street and household meal throughout the region.

Red Red (Beans Stew with Fried Plantain)

Ingredients:

  • Black-eyed beans
  • Red palm oil
  • Onions, tomatoes, pepper
  • Salt and seasonings
  • Fried ripe plantains
  • Smoked fish (optional)
  • Boil black-eyed beans until soft
  • Heat palm oil and fry chopped onions, tomatoes, and pepper
  • Add cooked beans and smoked fish (optional) and simmer
  • Season to taste
  • Serve with golden fried plantains

Origin Story:

Red Red is widely loved in Accra and across Ghana. The name "Red Red" comes from the red palm oil used in the stew and the golden-red hue of fried plantain. It reflects both indigenous Ga tastes and influences from other Southern communities. It’s a beloved vegetarian-friendly option that’s both simple and rich in flavor.

Kokonte with Groundnut Soup

Ingredients:

  • Dried cassava flour (kokonte)
  • Groundnuts (peanuts)
  • Tomatoes
  • Onions
  • Meat or fish
  • Salt and spices
  • Mix cassava flour with water and cook into a smooth dough
  • Roast and grind groundnuts into a paste
  • Blend onions and tomatoes
  • Cook meat/fish with blended ingredients
  • Add groundnut paste and simmer into a rich soup
  • Serve with kokonte

Origin Story:

Kokonte, also known as "Lafie" in Nzema and Ahanta communities, is a staple dish of the Western Region and surrounding forest belts. Once stigmatized as a “poverty food,” it is now widely accepted for its health benefits and is commonly served with rich, spicy soups like groundnut or palmnut soup.

Upper East Region Delicacies

Home to the Gurune (Frafra), Kasem, and Buli peoples, the Upper East Region features earthy, nutritious meals often based on millet, groundnuts, and local vegetables.

Tuo Zaafi with Bra (Green Leaf Soup)

Ingredients:

  • Millet flour
  • Bra leaves (baobab leaves or ayoyo)
  • Groundnut paste
  • Dawadawa (locust bean)
  • Onions and tomatoes
  • Smoked fish or bush meat
  • Salt and chili pepper
  • Mix millet flour with water and cook into a thick, smooth dough (T.Z)
  • Boil green leaves with dawadawa and smoked fish
  • Blend onions and tomatoes, add to soup
  • Add groundnut paste and let it simmer
  • Simmer until a slightly slimy, thick soup forms
  • Serve with tuo zaafi

Origin Story:

This version of Tuo Zaafi is common among the Frafra and Kusaasi people. The use of "Bra" (baobab or ayoyo leaves) symbolizes connection to the land, and millet, a drought-resistant crop, forms the staple for most rural families. It's a nutrient-rich, sustainable meal tied to traditional farming practices.

Rice Balls (Omo Tuo) with Groundnut Soup

Ingredients:

  • Rice
  • Groundnut paste (natural peanut butter)
  • Tomatoes
  • Onions and garlic
  • Salt
  • Spices and seasoning
  • Goat meat or guinea fowl
  • Boil rice until very soft, then mash and mold into balls
  • Cook meat with onions and spices
  • Add blended tomato, onion, and garlic to pot
  • Add groundnut paste and simmer until a rich, oily soup forms
  • Serve soup hot with rice balls

Origin Story:

This meal is popular in northern Ghana, including the Upper East. Groundnut soup is a delicacy among the Kasena and Builsa peoples. Guinea fowl is often used, symbolizing hospitality and status. Rice balls are a more modern twist replacing millet-based tuo for convenience.

Upper West Region Delicacies

The Dagaaba, Sissala, and Wala peoples of the Upper West Region bring hearty meals rooted in millet, sorghum, and shea-based cooking.

Saab (Millet Tuo) with Dry Okra Soup

Ingredients:

  • Millet flour
  • Dried okra powder
  • Groundnut paste
  • Dry or smoked fish/meat (goat, bush meat)
  • Salt and chili pepper
  • Dawadawa
  • Water
  • Mix millet flour with water and cook into a thick ball (Saab or tuo)
  • Boil meat or fish with dawadawa and seasonings
  • Mix groundnut paste with dried okra powder into boiling soup
  • Simmer until it thickens into a slightly mucilaginous texture
  • Serve with the millet ball

Origin Story:

This is a staple among the Dagaaba and Wala people. Dried okra adds body and a distinctive flavor to soups in this dry region. Millet, grown widely due to the arid conditions, forms the base of most meals. It's common during community festivals and traditional ceremonies.

Gablee (Sorghum Porridge)

Ingredients:

  • Sorghum flour
  • Water
  • Sugar or shea butter (optional)
  • Cloves or ginger (optional)
  • Mix sorghum flour in cold water and stir until smooth
  • Boil water and pour the mixture in while stirring continuously
  • Let it simmer into a thick porridge
  • Sweeten with sugar or shea butter
  • Serve hot, often for breakfast

Origin Story:

Gablee is a traditional porridge made from sorghum flour, enjoyed by the Wala and Sissala people. Sorghum is cultivated across the Upper West due to its resilience in dry climates. It is often flavored with local spices and served during market days or early morning gatherings.

Brong Ahafo Region Delicacies

Known for fertile lands and diverse food crops, the Brong Ahafo Region is home to the Bono, Ahafo, and Dormaa peoples. Their cuisine features plantain, yam, cocoyam, and fresh vegetables.

Ampesi (Boiled Yam/Plantain) with Nkontomire Stew

Ingredients:

  • Yam and/or ripe/unripe plantains
  • Nkontomire (cocoyam leaves)
  • Palm oil
  • Onions, tomatoes, and pepper
  • Salted fish (koobi) or smoked fish
  • Egusi (melon seeds) or groundnut paste (optional)
  • Salt and seasoning
  • Peel and boil yam or plantains in salted water
  • Boil washed cocoyam leaves separately
  • In a pan, heat palm oil and fry onions, tomatoes, pepper
  • Add fish and cooked leaves
  • Add ground melon seeds (optional) and simmer
  • Serve stew with boiled yam or plantain

Origin Story:

This dish reflects the Bono people's love for root-based meals with leafy stews. The use of nkontomire, grown in the region’s wetlands, shows the connection between agriculture and daily cuisine. Koobi adds a strong traditional flavor passed down through generations.

Fufu with Light Soup (Goat Meat or Snail)

Ingredients:

  • Boiled cassava and plantain (for pounding)
  • Goat meat or snails
  • Tomatoes, pepper, onions, garlic
  • Ginger, seasoning, and salt
  • Local herbs (optional)
  • Boil cassava and plantain, pound until smooth fufu forms
  • Season and boil meat or snails until tender
  • Blend tomatoes, pepper, onions, and garlic, then add to the meat
  • Simmer into a light, aromatic soup
  • Serve hot with fufu

Origin Story:

Fufu and light soup is a weekend favorite in the Ahafo area, often served to guests. Snails are a delicacy in forest zones, and the Bono people use local herbs for added aroma. Traditionally eaten in a shared bowl, it reflects unity and community hospitality.
