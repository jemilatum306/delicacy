A Taste of Ghana's Diversity

Ghana's culinary landscape is as diverse as its people. Each region boasts unique dishes shaped by local ingredients, cultural influences, and traditional cooking methods. This interactive guide showcases the most representative dishes from each of Ghana's regions.

Northern Region Delicacies

Discover some popular dishes amomg the Dagomba's and other ethnic groups in the north.

Tuo Zaafi with Ayoyo stew

Ingredients

  • Corn flour or millet flour
  • Jute leaves (Ayoyo)
  • Dawadawa (local seasoning)
  • Peppers
  • Onions
  • Tomatoes
  • Meat or fish
  • Salt
  • Mix corn flour with water to form a smooth paste
  • Cook while stirring continuously until thickened (Tuo zaafi)
  • Wash and chop jute leaves
  • Boil meat or fish with dawadawa seasioning
  • Blend onions , tomatoes and pepper
  • Combine blended ingredients, mix with cooked meat and jute leaves
  • Simmer until soup thickens

Origin Story:

Tuo Zaafi, also known as T.Z., originates from the Hausa language. The name itself combines "Tuo," meaning stirring or paddling, and "Zaafi," meaning hot. It is a popular dish in Northern Ghana, and also widely enjoyed in other West African countries like Nigeria, Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger. Tuo Zaafi is considered a nutritious and easily digestible meal.

Wasawasa

Ingredients

  • Yam
  • Pepper
  • Onions
  • Salt
  • Tomatoes
  • Eggs (optional)
  • Peel and grate raw yam
  • Allow grated yam to ferment for 1-2 days
  • Mix fermented yam with salt
  • Scoop into portions and steam until set
  • Prepare sauce with blended peppers , onions and tomatoes
  • Serve wasawasa with sauce

Origin Story:

Wasawasa, a traditional dish from northern Ghana, particularly among the Dagbon people, is made from dried yam peels ground into flour and steamed. Its origin story is rooted in resourcefulness and a desire to minimize food waste. A legend says that a woman, initially throwing away yam peels, realized they could be used to make a nutritious meal, thus creating wasawasa.

Ashanti Delicacies

Home to legendary dishes like fufu and light soup with rich, comforting flavors.

Fufu and light soup

What You'll Need:

  • Plantains and cassava (for fufu)
  • Tomatoes
  • Onions
  • Garlic
  • Ginger
  • Garden eggs
  • Meat (goat,beef,or fish)
  • Salt and spices
  • Peel and boil plantains and cassava until soft
  • Pound in a mortar to make smooth fufu dough
  • Blend tomatoes, onions, garlic, and ginger for the soup base
  • Cook meat with spices until tender
  • Combine blended ingredients with meat and simmer
  • Serve fufu with soup in a bowl

The Story Behind This Dish:

The word "Fufu" is believed to be derived from the Twi language, meaning "white," likely referring to the whitish color of the cassava component. Fufu, a starchy dough, is traditionally made by pounding boiled cassava and plantains together. Light Soup, a flavorful broth, is known for its thin consistency and is traditionally tomato-based, often featuring seafood. The dish is a staple, especially for Sunday meals, and is enjoyed by many across Ghana and beyond.

Nkontomire (Palava Sauce)

What You'll Need:

  • Cocoyam leaves (kontomire)
  • Palm oil
  • Onions
  • Tomatoes
  • Eggs
  • Fish (smoked or salted)
  • Peppers
  • Salt and spices
  • Wash and chop cocoyam leaves finely
  • Boil eggs and set aside
  • Fry onions, tomatoes, and peppers in palm oil
  • Add chopped leaves and cook until tender
  • Add flaked fish and boiled eggs
  • Season to taste and simmer for flavors to blend

The Story Behind This Dish:

Kontomire stew, also known as Palava Sauce, is a popular Ghanaian dish made with cocoyam leaves (kontomire), often featuring a mix of meats, smoked fish, and other ingredients. The name "Palava Sauce" is said to have originated in Elmina, Ghana, and is thought to be linked to the English word "palaver," referring to a lively or even argumentative discussion. The dish is known for its rich, flavorful taste and is commonly enjoyed with various sides like boiled yam, plantain, and rice. Kontomire stew can be made with different variations, such as adding agushi seeds, or with different types of fish. It's a versatile dish that can be adapted to individual preferences and regional variations.

Mpoto Mpoto (Yam Pottage)

What You'll Need:

  • Yam
  • Palm oil
  • Onions
  • Tomatoes
  • Peppers
  • Fish or meat
  • Salt and spices
  • Peel and cut yam into chunks
  • Boil yam until soft but not mushy
  • Prepare sauce separately with palm oil, onions, tomatoes, and peppers
  • Add fish or meat to sauce and simmer
  • Add boiled yam chunks to sauce and mash slightly
  • Cook together briefly until flavors meld

The Story Behind This Dish:

Mpoto Mpoto, also known as Yam Pottage or Asaro, is a popular Ghanaian dish, particularly among the Akan people. It's a simple yet flavorful one-pot meal made with yam or cocoyam, often cooked with palm oil, onions, peppers, and various spices, sometimes including smoked fish. The dish is known for its creamy texture and is enjoyed hot or warm.

Central Region Delicacies

Specializes in fresh seafood dishes and coastal flavors.

Kenkey and Fried Fish

Ingredients:

  • Corn dough
  • Fish (typically tilapia)
  • Peppers
  • Onions
  • Tomatoes
  • Salt
  • Oil for frying
  • Dry banana leaves for wrapping
  • Ferment corn dough partially
  • Mix portion of dough with boiling water to form 'kata'
  • Combine with remaining dough and wrap in banana leaves
  • Steam wrapped dough for several hours
  • Clean and season fish
  • Prepare hot pepper sauce with onions and tomatoes
  • Fry fish until crispy

Origin Story:

Kenkey and fried fish is a classic Ghanaian dish, with kenkey (also known as kɔmi or dokono) originating from the Ga and Fante communities of Ghana. Kenkey is a type of fermented corn dough dumpling, and fried fish is a common accompaniment, often paired with a spicy pepper sauce (shito).

Ampesi with Kontomire

Ingredients:

  • Cocoyam leaves
  • Yam
  • Plantains
  • Palm oil
  • Onions
  • Peppers
  • Smoked fish
  • Salt
  • Peel and cut yam and plantains into chunks
  • Boil in salted water until tender
  • Wash and chop cocoyam leaves
  • Sauté onions and peppers in palm oil
  • Add chopped leaves and cook until tender
  • Flake smoked fish into sauce
  • Serve boiled yam and plantains with kontomire sauce

Origin Story:

Ampesi with Kontomire is a traditional Ghanaian dish with roots in the Akan culture. Ampesi refers to the boiled starchy foods like plantain, yam, or cocoyam, while Kontomire is a stew made from cocoyam leaves (taro leaves). The dish is a staple in many Ghanaian homes and is known for its nutritional value and simple preparation.

Volta Region Delicacies

Discover features staples like Akple and traditional Ewe dishes.

Akple with Okro Soup

What You'll Need:

  • Corn dough
  • Cassava dough
  • Okro (okra)
  • Fish or Crab(optional)
  • Onions and pepper
  • Palm oil
  • Salt
  • Mix corn and cassava dough with water
  • Cook mixture into stiff paste (Akple)
  • Slice okro into small pieces
  • Prepare fish and crab
  • Sauté onions and peppers in palm oil
  • Add okro, fish and crab with some water
  • Cook until slimy consistency is achieved

The Story Behind This Dish:

Akple with okra soup is a traditional Ghanaian dish originating from the Ewe people of the Volta Region. The Ewe are known for this dish, which is also enjoyed by other ethnic groups in Ghana and parts of Togo. Akple is made from a dough of fermented corn and cassava flour, while okra soup is a flavorful stew made with okra pods, vegetables, and often meat or fish. The combination of Akple and okra soup is a staple at Ewe cultural events and family gatherings.

Dzenkple

What You'll Need:

  • Corn flour
  • Smoked fish
  • Shrimp and crap
  • Tomatoes and onions
  • Ginger and garlic
  • Salt and seasoning
  • Coconut milk
  • The stew is made by sautéing onions and other aromatics, then adding blended tomatoes, tomato paste, and the spice blend.
  • Shrimp and crabs are often seasoned and cooked separately or added directly to the stew.
  • The corn flour is added to the stew, often after it has been cooked down and thickened, and stirred until it forms a porridge-like consistency.
  • Coconut milk, water, and additional seasonings are added to the dzenkple before serving.

The Story Behind This Dish:

Dzenkple is a traditional Ghanaian dish, particularly popular among the Ewe people of the Volta Region and neighboring areas in Togo, Benin, and Nigeria. It's a festive meal featuring roasted corn flour cooked in a rich tomato stew, often mistaken for Aprapransa, which uses palmnut soup. Dzenkple is known for its distinctive tomato-based stew.

Yakeyake

What You'll Need

  • Cassava
  • Salt
  • Corn flour (optional)
  • Pepper/stew or soup
  • Makaya leaves(optional)
  • Peel and grate the cassava. Some recipes suggest soaking the grated cassava to remove toxins.
  • The grated cassava is then squeezed or pressed to remove excess water.
  • Salt is added to the grated cassava, and the mixture is steamed, traditionally using a muslin cloth or a special earthenware bowl with holes.
  • Once cooked, Yakeyake is often served with a stew, pepper sauce, or a fish broth

The Story Behind The Dish

Yakeyake, a steamed cassava dish, is a culinary staple primarily associated with the Ewe people of the Volta Region in Ghana. It's a testament to the region's rich culinary heritage and is known for its unique preparation method and cultural significance.
