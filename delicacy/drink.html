Signature Drinks

Authentic recipes that have stood the test of time

Northern Specialty

Pito

The Soul of Northern Ghana

This mildly alcoholic brew made from fermented millet or sorghum has been central to Dagomba and Frafra celebrations for centuries, served in hand-carved calabashes.

Traditional Preparation

  1. Malting: Grains are soaked, germinated, and sun-dried to develop enzymes
  2. Mashing: Malted grains are mixed with water and heated to convert starches
  3. Straining: The liquid is filtered through woven grass sieves
  4. Fermentation: Natural yeast works for 24-48 hours in earthenware pots
  5. Serving: Poured warm from the brewing pot into calabash cups
Taste Notes: Earthy malt | Tangy fermentation | Subtle sweetness
National Favorite

Sobolo

Ghana's Ruby Refreshment

This vibrant hibiscus infusion transforms simple ingredients into a beverage that's equally at home in royal palaces and street-side chop bars across the nation.

Traditional Preparation

  1. Selection: Premium dried hibiscus petals are carefully sorted
  2. Infusion: Petals simmer with ginger, cloves, and sometimes pineapple rinds
  3. Straining: Liquid is filtered through fine muslin cloth
  4. Sweetening: Balanced with raw cane sugar or local honey
  5. Presentation: Served over ice in glass bottles with pineapple garnish
Taste Notes: Tart cranberry | Spicy ginger | Floral finish
Street Classic

Asaana

Southern Ghana's Sweet Staple

This nourishing fermented corn drink bridges generations, equally beloved by schoolchildren and market traders seeking refreshment under the tropical sun.

Traditional Preparation

  1. Soaking: White maize kernels soak for 2-3 days to soften
  2. Blending: Fermented corn is mixed with fresh water
  3. Straining: Pulp is removed through fine mesh sieves
  4. Flavoring: Fresh ginger and sugar are added to taste
  5. Chilling: Served ice-cold in recycled glass bottles
Taste Notes: Creamy texture | Mild tang | Ginger spice
Artisanal Spirit

Akpeteshie or Palm wine

Ghana's Forbidden Spirit

Born from colonial resistance, this potent palm or sugarcane distillate has evolved from underground moonshine to a celebrated cultural icon.

Traditional Preparation

  1. Harvesting: Palm sap or sugarcane juice is collected fresh
  2. Fermentation: Natural yeasts work for 3-5 days in wooden vats
  3. Distillation: Liquid is heated in repurposed oil drums with copper coils
  4. Proofing: Master distillers test alcohol content by flame
  5. Aging: Premium versions rest in oak barrels for smoothness
Taste Notes: Fiery intensity | Fruity undertones | Lingering warmth
Milky Delight

Burkina

Northern Ghana’s Creamy Chill

Burkina, a cooling millet and milk beverage, blends the nourishing grains of the north with creamy dairy. It’s a street-vendor favorite in Ghana’s cities and a go-to for breakfast or post-meal refreshment.

Traditional Preparation

  1. Soaking: Pearl millet is soaked overnight until soft
  2. Cooking: Millet is boiled until tender and slightly mushy
  3. Cooling: Cooked grains are cooled to room temperature
  4. Mixing: Evaporated or powdered milk is blended in
  5. Sweetening: Sugar is added and it’s chilled before serving
Taste Notes: Creamy texture | Mildly sweet | Nutty grain flavor
Zesty Herbal

Lamugine

Northern Ghana’s Wellness Brew

Lamugin, or Hausa beer, is a natural energy drink packed with ginger and healing spices. It's sold from large coolers on Ghana’s streets and is known for its medicinal and cooling properties.

Traditional Preparation

  1. Grating: Fresh ginger root is finely grated
  2. Blending: Blended with cloves, pepper, and tamarind or lime juice
  3. Boiling: The mixture is simmered to intensify flavor
  4. Cooling: Left to cool before adding sugar
  5. Straining: Filtered and served cold in bottles
Taste Notes: Sharp ginger | Citrus tang | Spicy warmth
Coastal Classic

Mashed Kenkey

Ga Mashie's Summer Cooler

This refreshing smoothie made from kenkey is a street favorite in Accra. It’s popular among the youth and often enjoyed with roasted groundnuts or bread.

Traditional Preparation

  1. Mashing: Kenkey is crumbled or blended with cold water
  2. Mixing: Sugar and a pinch of salt are stirred in
  3. Flavoring: Often served with a dash of lemon or milk
  4. Serving: Poured over ice cubes in plastic cups
  5. Pairing: Eaten with groundnuts or bread for extra energy
Taste Notes: Tangy base | Slight saltiness | Subtle sour-sweet balance
